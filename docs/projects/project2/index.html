The Problem with Propensity Targeting

Most marketing models ask: who is most likely to convert? That sounds right — but it's the wrong question. Targeting high-propensity users wastes budget on two groups: people who would have converted anyway (Sure Things), and people who actually respond negatively to outreach (Sleeping Dogs).

Uplift modeling reframes the question as: for whom does the treatment cause conversion? This is a causal inference problem. The goal is to estimate the individual treatment effect — the difference in conversion probability between a user who receives the action and the same user in a counterfactual world where they don't.

Maximizing conversion rate is not the same as maximizing profit.

Because we can never observe both outcomes for the same person, uplift models are trained on randomized control trial data — where treatment assignment is independent of user characteristics — and the target is the estimated difference in outcomes, not the outcome itself.

The Four Customer Segments

Persuadables

Convert if treated, don't if untreated. These are the only users where the treatment creates genuine incremental value.

Target
Sure Things

Convert regardless of treatment. Targeting them wastes spend without any lift in outcome.

Skip
Lost Causes

Won't convert regardless. No treatment will change this — budget is better spent elsewhere.

Skip
Sleeping Dogs

Less likely to convert if treated. Outreach actively hurts conversion — these users must be excluded.

What I'd Do Differently

Add Cost Constraints

The current model optimizes for uplift score alone. A production version should incorporate send cost and expected revenue per conversion to solve for the optimal targeting budget, not just the optimal segment boundary.

Try the X-Learner

The T-learner treats treatment and control models independently. The X-learner cross-fits the residuals, which tends to reduce variance when treatment and control sample sizes are unequal — common in real campaigns.

Calibrate Probabilities

Tree-based models like XGBoost output well-ranked but poorly calibrated probabilities. Applying Platt scaling or isotonic regression before taking the difference would produce uplift scores that are meaningful in absolute terms, not just rank order.
